Project Idea

Overview

The global COVID-19 pandemic has put surface disinfectants to the test. It has also prompted organizations to develop ultraviolet-disinfection or otherwise known as UV-C robots. These robots are becoming more popular as an option for disinfecting rooms and spaces of all surfaces in one process, and as a result, they appear to be appealing to hospital administrators. This is what I have decided for my project to be as not only is it useful but also less costly then typical manpower. Furthermore according to R&D, UVC Robots improve sanitisation by 60% compared to liquid based cleaning.

Motivation

This project to me is very useful as first of all, it would give me some experience of the key skills required for my dream job. Not only this but as stated above it would also fill the desires of my mum as i technically am working in the hospital. The main motivation behind this however is the need to do something for people. This is a genuine and sad fact that people mostly go to hospital as a way of obtaining medicine or treatment for their illness. Healthcare-associated infections (HAIs) are complications of healthcare and linked with high morbidity and mortality. Each year, about 1 in 25 U.S. hospital patients is diagnosed with at least one infection related to hospital care alone. 1 in 25 !!! Can you believe this? For those who even go to a hospital or even a medical clinic for just a regular checkup could come back contracted with the worst of bacteria/infections. Some of the most common infections people transmit across medical clinics include but are not limited to : UTI, pneumonia and even lung infections. The need to help stop this due to lack of staff or lack of care provided, I feel that UV-C robots are the perfect way to transform our hospitals in the future if we genuinely want to save lives.

Description

The robot moves automatically in a defined area, after being programmed for things like time and distance from surfaces. It is made up of multiple lamps placed on top of a platform. During disinfection, the Robots emit UV-C irradiation at a wavelength of around 200-300 nanometres, allowing for a full 360 degree coverage. During the disinfection process, the robot moves at a steady speed, achieving a coverage of areas at a distance of several meters. However, in order to enable auto moving, the robot must be programmed via the use of a detailed map/blueprint of the hospital or medical clinic. Once every aspect is set, furniture and all other objects must remain in exactly the same place to enable an autonomous functioning. Currently, according to my idea and also for safety, this UV-C robot automatically shuts off when its motion sensor detects any moving things (whether it's a human or a vase falling down) during the disinfection process. Despite the ultimate recommendations, manual cleaning in each hospital is based mostly on local protocol and cleaning staff’s personal opinions, as well as the control and the inspection of their performance. Evidence further suggests that manual cleaning and disinfection are often inadequate and result in contamination (which we know to be true). Besides killing microorganisms on surfaces, disinfection robots automatically record the operation parameters of the disinfection process and by this, can provide efficiency and effectiveness assurance. Advantages of these robots also include but aren’t limited to : disinfection will work in an unmanned manner, without the need for ongoing human presence at the disinfection site. Therefore, exposure of health care workers to harmful UV radiation can be largely avoided during the process.Applying UV-C as a final disinfection step after manual disinfection provides an additional hygiene benefit to reducing cross-transmission and associated infections which I mentioned above. UV light does not leave any residues, making this an environmentally friendly disinfection method. However with every innovation comes limitations (My own quote, might’ve taken some inspiration from the spider man quote “With great power, comes great responsibility”). Limitations that I think this project would have is the need for some sort of pre-cleaning for the program to fully work during the initial launch and also length of time and affect to safety regulations.

Business Aspect

If you’ve read all of the above, you might be thinking how innovative this is. Well my business model is simple, instead of making obnoxiously sized and pricey robots, I would rather be making miniature models of the same project for a less costly price. Therefore money input towards the project < total profit. So for example instead of a hospital purchasing a large machine to clean up a smaller surface area, they could purchase 2 maybe 3 of my smaller models to clean up the room which would not only be effective but also efficient for both me to make money and the hospital to save money. Research suggests that the production cost of each robot without labor could easily cost $50+ per robot. Now if I were to produce one that's ⅓ of the size of an obnoxious one, you would estimate that my production cost would be around $15. Now those companies making their robots for $50+ are selling their product for around $600, which hospitals are hesitant to buy as man power per hour is cheaper. However the aspect most hospitals don’t realize is that the purchase of a robot is permanent whereas staff changes require more or less depending. If I were to turn this idea into a business, I'd potentially look to profit at least $75 per robot, meaning selling the robot that cost me $15 for $100. Now think to yourself, would companies buy 1 large robot for $600+ or 1 for $100 and add on as many as they want based on their demands. Not only this but this would speed up the process by around 50% as there could be robots working in multiple rooms at a time.
